## E2
### 2.1 Weak Formulation

**Steps:**
1.  **Multiply** the momentum equation by a test function $\mathbf{v} \in V$ and the continuity equation by a test function $q \in Q$.
2.  **Integrate** over the domain $\Omega$.
3.  **Apply Green's formula** (integration by parts) to the Laplacian and Pressure Gradient terms:
    $$-\int_{\Omega} \mu \Delta \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = \int_{\Omega} \mu \nabla \mathbf{u} : \nabla \mathbf{v} - \int_{\partial \Omega} \mu \frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial n} \cdot \mathbf{v}$$
    $$\int_{\Omega} \nabla p \cdot \mathbf{v} = -\int_{\Omega} p \, \text{div} \mathbf{v} + \int_{\partial \Omega} p \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{v}$$
4.  **Substitute the boundary condition:** $\mu \frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial n} - p \mathbf{n} = \mathbf{h}$ on $\partial \Omega$.
    The boundary terms combine as:
    $$-\int_{\partial \Omega} \left( \mu \frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial n} - p \mathbf{n} \right) \cdot \mathbf{v} = -\int_{\partial \Omega} \mathbf{h} \cdot \mathbf{v}$$

**Solution:**
Find $\mathbf{u} \in H^1(\Omega)^2$ and $p \in L^2(\Omega)$ such that for all $\mathbf{v} \in H^1(\Omega)^2$ and $q \in L^2(\Omega)$:

$$
\begin{cases}
a(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}) + b(\mathbf{v}, p) = F(\mathbf{v}) \\
b(\mathbf{u}, q) = 0
\end{cases}
$$

Where the bilinear forms and linear functional are:
* $a(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}) = \int_{\Omega} (\mu \nabla \mathbf{u} : \nabla \mathbf{v} + \alpha \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}) \, d\Omega$
* $b(\mathbf{v}, p) = -\int_{\Omega} p \, \text{div} \mathbf{v} \, d\Omega$
* $F(\mathbf{v}) = \int_{\partial \Omega} \mathbf{h} \cdot \mathbf{v} \, ds$

---

### 2.2 Taylor-Hood Approximation

**Approximation:**
We introduce a triangulation $\mathcal{T}_h$ of the domain. We choose the **Taylor-Hood** finite element spaces $V_h$ and $Q_h$:
* **Velocity ($\mathbf{u}_h$):** Continuous piecewise polynomials of degree $k$ ($\mathbb{P}_k$).
* **Pressure ($p_h$):** Continuous piecewise polynomials of degree $k-1$ ($\mathbb{P}_{k-1}$).

**Discrete Problem:**
Find $\mathbf{u}_h \in V_h$ and $p_h \in Q_h$ such that:
$$
\begin{cases}
\int_{\Omega} (\mu \nabla \mathbf{u}_h : \nabla \mathbf{v}_h + \alpha \mathbf{u}_h \cdot \mathbf{v}_h) - \int_{\Omega} p_h \, \text{div} \mathbf{v}_h = \int_{\partial \Omega} \mathbf{h} \cdot \mathbf{v}_h & \forall \mathbf{v}_h \in V_h \\
\int_{\Omega} q_h \, \text{div} \mathbf{u}_h = 0 & \forall q_h \in Q_h
\end{cases}
$$

---

### 2.3 Stability of Approximation

**Answer:** **Yes**, the approximation is stable.

**Explanation:**
The Taylor-Hood element pair ($\mathbb{P}_k / \mathbb{P}_{k-1}$ with $k \ge 2$) satisfies the **inf-sup condition** (also known as the **LBB condition** â€” Ladyzhenskaya-Babuska-Brezzi condition). This condition ensures that the velocity space is sufficiently "rich" to control the pressure, preventing numerical instabilities and ensuring a unique solution (up to a hydrostatic constant if applicable).

---

### 2.4 Example of Unstable Spaces

**Example:**
The **Equal-Order $\mathbb{P}_1 / \mathbb{P}_1$** (or $\mathbb{Q}_1 / \mathbb{Q}_1$) approximation is **not** stable.

Using linear polynomials for both velocity and pressure violates the inf-sup condition. This lack of stability often leads to spurious pressure oscillations (checkerboard patterns) because the discrete gradient operator has a non-trivial kernel (spurious modes).

---

### 2.5 Error Estimate and Spurious Modes

**Error Estimate:**
For the Taylor-Hood elements of degree $k$ and $k-1$, the optimal convergence rates are:
* **Velocity:** $\| \mathbf{u} - \mathbf{u}_h \|_{H^1} \le C h^k$ (and $\| \mathbf{u} - \mathbf{u}_h \|_{L^2} \le C h^{k+1}$)
* **Pressure:** $\| p - p_h \|_{L^2} \le C h^k$

**(Note: The overall convergence of the mixed problem is generally dictated by the lower order, so $O(h^k)$ in the energy norm).**

**Spurious Modes:**
* **Expectation:** **No**, we do not expect spurious modes for this approximation.
* **Motivation:** Because the Taylor-Hood elements satisfy the inf-sup condition, the formulation is well-posed and stable.
* **Definition of Spurious Mode:** A spurious mode is a non-physical solution to the discrete problem, typically manifesting as high-frequency oscillations in the pressure field (e.g., checkerboard instability). These occur when the pressure space is too large relative to the velocity space, allowing non-constant pressure fields $p_h$ to satisfy $\int p_h \text{div} \mathbf{v}_h = 0$ for all discrete velocities, effectively "hiding" from the continuity equation constraint.
